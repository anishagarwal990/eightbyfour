Choosing the Right Plywood Grade for Your Project

Grade is the first decision, and the one most likely to go wrong. Four letters do the real work — MR, BWR, BWP, and FR — and mixing them up is how a wardrobe swells in a monsoon or a kitchen cabinet delaminates over a sink.

MR (Moisture Resistant) is the default for normal interior use — cabinets, wardrobes, and furniture in dry rooms. It isn't built for standing water or constant humidity, so keep it out of bathrooms and kitchens.

BWR (Boil Water Resistant) sits a step up, tolerating occasional splashes and damp conditions without being rated for continuous water contact.

BWP (Boiling Water Proof) is the highest bonding standard in plywood, tested by boiling. It's the standard choice for kitchens, bathrooms, and anywhere moisture is a daily fact of life, not an occasional one.

FR (Fire Retardant) is treated to slow the spread of flame, and shows up most often in commercial interiors, public buildings, and any space with stricter fire-safety requirements.

As a rule of thumb: match the grade to the room, not the price tag. If you're unsure which grade your project needs, tell us the room and the use case when you inquire, and we'll help you land on the right one before you order.

Reference

Material & Grade Notes

What does MR grade mean?

MR stands for Moisture Resistant. It suits normal interior use — cabinets, wardrobes, and furniture in dry areas — but isn't built for standing water or continuous humidity.

What does BWP grade mean?

BWP stands for Boiling Water Proof, the highest bonding standard for plywood. It's tested by boiling and is typically used in kitchens, bathrooms, and other high-moisture areas.

What's the difference between BWP and BWR?

BWR (Boil Water Resistant) sits between MR and BWP — it tolerates occasional moisture exposure but isn't rated for the constant water contact that BWP handles.

What does FR grade mean?

FR stands for Fire Retardant — treated to slow the spread of flame. It's common in commercial interiors, public buildings, and areas with stricter fire-safety requirements.

What's the standard plywood sheet size?

8×4 ft (96×48 in) is the industry-standard sheet size, stocked as the default by most manufacturers. Other sizes are typically available on request.

Which thickness should I choose?

As a general guide: thinner sheets (4–6mm) suit backs and shutters, mid-range (9–12mm) suits general cabinetry, and 18–19mm+ suits load-bearing panels like shelving. Confirm exact requirements with your fabricator, or ask us via Inquire.

What is HPL (High Pressure Laminate)?

HPL is a decorative sheet made of resin-impregnated layers fused under high pressure and heat, then bonded onto plywood, MDF, or particle board. It's the standard surface finish for cabinet doors, wardrobes, and countertops.

What's a matching edge band for?

An edge band is a thin strip applied to the exposed edge of a panel to hide the raw core material and give it a finished look. Ordering it color-matched to the laminate sheet keeps the visible edge and face consistent.
